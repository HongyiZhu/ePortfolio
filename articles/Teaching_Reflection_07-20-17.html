Reflection - Student Engagement

07/20/2017

Lesson plan can be found here: Lesson_Plan_07-18-17

The focus of this article is student engagement. Especially when the topic is very concept driven and the concepts are less relevant to students' daily life.

In order to engage the students, I designed concept recall, pair discussion, and video watching activities in this lesson. I want to discuss my findings of the pair discussion activity and the use of video in-class here.

Pair Discussion Activity

The task for pair discussion was to come up with possible waste in the production processes. I asked the students to define "waste" themselves and indicate the part of the production process that might produce/lead to their defined "waste." To help them define waste, I provided some potential directions and an example of time waste in the production. I allocated three minutes for the discussion.

The students took 20 seconds to understand my instruction and started the discussion. They stopped before the time ran out. I thought they might have conclusions already. However, when I asked volunteers to share their answers, the classroom was very silent.

Pair/Group discussion is a tool to engage students learning actively. However, in my process, I did not clearly mention about my result sharing expectation in the discussion instruction. The discussion/sharing activity could have been more engaging if I mentioned each pair should come up with one example to share. Then I could point to any discussion pairs and they would be prepared.

I will explicitly mention my expectation of discussion result sharing in my future pair/group discussion activities. I want to see how will they react when I push them a little bit more.

Youtube Video Use In-class

Since this session has a lot of concepts that the students are not familiar with, I used many Youtube videos either to relate the concept to student's daily life or to show them how those concepts are applied in real factory productions. I expect students will be more engaged if they can watch the video with vivid examples. However, students were not as engaged as I expected.

I tried two ways to use the video. For the first one, I explained the concept first and then show the video about real production. Students were not very engaged this way. Some of them started to read their own laptop screens and stopped following the class.

For the other way, I posted video related questions for students to answer. Students were more engaged in watching the video and answering questions.

My conclusion for the video use in-class is that we can always give specific instructions for students and clearly explain the purpose of the video before they watch the video.
